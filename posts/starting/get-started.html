    Starting a blog

    By Joy Yang | 120.121.2020

    No title

    Hi, I am Joy Yang and I am starting this blog. I plan to publish at least once a month. My posts will be mostly about interesting Mathematical problems. Sometimes, there will be some posts involving computing, expect heterogeneous computation topics like GPGPU. As inspired by Francis Bach, I plan to release every first Monday of the month. Overall, I hope this blog can be helpful.

    1Why Math and how to interpret them?

    This is actually a question that I have been thinking ever since I read the paper \(A^{\ast}\) sampling – it is a beautiful algorithm with theoretical guarantees, but what is the use case of this algorithm. This problem constantly frustrates me, because I feel that taking a lot of effort in reading papers helps so little in my research. Even now, I don't know when I will ever use this algorithm. In other words, I cannot justify spending time and energy reading papers. Sometimes, I feel stupid to spend huge amount of time on something that does not directly benefit me. It feels like that I use this time for playing video games (or just to have fun), but with a purpose of actually putting it into practical use. But when I look back, this has never been my first “fail” investment, which raises another problem hidden in plain sight – why do I keep doing this and should I keep doing this?

    As it turns out, Grant Sanderson has figured it out for me, at least partially. In his talk, titled “What Makes People Engage With Math?”, he talked about why the most viewed videos of his channel are not something as practical as “Nerual Network” or “Fourier Transform”. The reason behind this, and he made a brilliant analogy, “when reading Harry Potter, when will I ever use Wingardium Leviosa?”, is similar to the reason people read novel and fiction. “It's about emotion, it's about wonder, and it's about a mystery that you just need to see resolved.” This inspires me, working out Mathematical problems is not that much different from reading other forms of materials. It attracts you from another angle. Peeling off the obscure veil of Mathematical symbols, it prompts you a question that you have to see resolved. The more peculiar the problem is, the more engagement one will put in. This is one of the reasons I keep pushing myself into these pits.

    On the other hand, I have to admit, my initial attempt at this line of research is due to it is hard, and if it is hard, then I would outsmart other people, then I would prove my value in the community. This dangerous thought has led me into a rabbit hole. In retrospect, no one would ever deliberately take on a career just because it is challenging. Everyone wants a simple and happy life. Only a fool like me would do that just to outsmart other people. Then again, it is a tempting thought. Gradually, I realize that scientific research is about sharing, not showing off. When presented with a difficult path and a simple path, I should take the simple path. This means that if I encounter a paper or other media that is hard to follow through and the problem it's trying to solve doesn't attract me enough, I should stop. I shouldn't read it because it's hard.

    Another problem I find in reading papers is that I only seek to understand the symbols of equations, or I just derive of how equations come up, but I don't look for deeper understanding, or how I would describe this paper without mathematical notations, i.e., what is the intuition behind all these complicated symbols. This is a serious problem I uncover lately. I am always trying to interpret problems in math symbols, which are the most obscure things ever created by human. No one talks or thinks like that! We use them because it serves the purpose – it is precise, it causes no ambiguity. But at the same time, they thwart people from accessing the true nature of the problem. Now, whenever I read a paper, I should form a hierarchy of understanding. At the bottom, I should know how each equation is derived. At the top, I should describe the main idea in plain language, where it is easy for me to think and work on. And I should emphasize more on the high level part, whlie, sometimes minor mathematical details should be ignored. What is funny about the phrase “deep understanding” is that it is actually not anything deep. Instead, it is usually a simple idea extracted from all the mass presented to you (usually a paper).

    Reading a paper is like taking a treat from a tall shelf. I should consider how to balance between how hard I should try, and whether it is worthy or not. Besides, I would put more focus on deeper understanding rather than mathematical notations. Those notations are merely means of transferring knowledge. What's important is the knowledge underneath them.
